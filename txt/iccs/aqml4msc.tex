\section{Introduction}
% Multisource
Data about an observed object can be gathered from multiple complementary sensors.
To obtain richer and more accurate decision outcomes, multisource information fusion (MSIF) can be employed.
A comprehensive review of information fusion approaches is provided by Li et al.~\cite{li_multi-source_2024}.
In this work, we focus on feature-level fusion, in which the fusion model operates on extracted feature representations.
MSIF has found widespread application in machine learning, particularly in classification tasks, as surveyed in~\cite{baltrusaitis_multimodal_2019}.
Representative applications include remote sensing~\cite{li_a3clnn_2022}, military systems~\cite{sycara_integrated_2009}, and human action recognition~\cite{fortino_advances_2019},
among others~\cite{li_multi-source_2024}.

% Transition: MSIF -> QML
Despite the success of classical machine learning models for feature-level fusion, their performance can be limited by model capacity and scaling issues in high-dimensional feature spaces.
This motivates the exploration of alternative computational paradigms for multisource learning.

% QML
Quantum machine learning (QML) leverages principles of quantum mechanics to process information in ways that differ fundamentally from classical machine learning~\cite{biamonte_quantum_2017}.
Several works have demonstrated that quantum models can represent complex functions using comparatively shallow circuits or fewer parameters than their classical counterparts,
suggesting potential advantages for learning tasks involving structured or high-dimensional data~\cite{havlicek_supervised_2019,schuld_quantum_2019,abbas_power_2021}.
Moreover, theoretical work has identified learning scenarios in which quantum models offer data-dependent advantages~\cite{huang_power_2021}.

% VQA on NISQ 
As fault-tolerant quantum computers have not yet been realized, practical QML research focuses on algorithms compatible with current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices~\cite{chen_complexity_2023}.
Among these, variational quantum algorithms (VQAs)~\cite{cerezo_variational_2021} are particularly prominent.
VQAs are hybrid quantum--classical methods that employ a classical optimizer to train a parameterized quantum circuit.
Importantly, Schuld et al.~\cite{schuld_circuit-centric_2020} have shown that such models exhibit robustness to noise in both input data and circuit parameters, making them suitable for near-term quantum hardware.

% QMSC
Recently, several works have begun to explore the application of quantum machine learning methods to multisource or multimodal data fusion.
Feature-level multimodal quantum fusion presented in pre-print from 2026~\cite{wu_feature_2026} takes features extracted by sperate unimodal classical neural networks.
Extracted features are embedded into quantum fusion circuit and entangled between multiple modalities and target qubits, with trainable parameters.
Their model showed consistent stability and high accuracy for high-dimensional data.

\section{Problem Formulation} \label{sec:problem_formulation}
We consider a synthetic multisource classification setting, where multiple input sources
are derived from a single original sample.
Specifically, an input image is partitioned into two complementary views.
Each view is independently transformed by a deterministic preprocessing pipeline
into a vector representation, denoted as $x_{\mathrm{top}}$ and $x_{\mathrm{bottom}}$,
which constitute the synthetic multisource inputs used for classification.

The vector inputs are subsequently passed through learnable feature extraction
functions $f(\cdot,\theta_1)$ and $f(\cdot,\theta_2)$, producing feature vectors
$v_{\mathrm{top}}$ and $v_{\mathrm{bottom}}$ of equal dimensionality.
These features are fused and classified by a function $h(\cdot,\theta_3)$,
yielding the final classification decision $d$.
The resulting formulation is defined as:
\begin{align*}
	v_{\mathrm{top}}    & = f(x_{\mathrm{top}}, \theta_1),                      \\
	v_{\mathrm{bottom}} & = f(x_{\mathrm{bottom}}, \theta_2),                   \\
	d                   & = h(v_{\mathrm{top}}, v_{\mathrm{bottom}}, \theta_3).
\end{align*}

All learnable parameters $\theta_1$, $\theta_2$, and $\theta_3$ are optimized jointly
during training.

\section{Synthetic Multisource Data Construction}
The MNIST (Modified National Institute of Standards and Technology) dataset consists of
70,000 images of handwritten digits (0--9), each of size $28 \times 28$ pixels.
The dataset was obtained from Kaggle \cite{kaggle_dataset},
and we used the predefined training--validation split provided with the dataset without modification.

From the dataset, we select only three digit classes:
\begin{itemize}
	\item digit ``5'' (\(5421\) training samples and \(892\) validation samples),
	\item digit ``6'' (\(5918\) training samples and \(958\) validation samples),
	\item digit ``7'' (\(6265\) training samples and \(1028\) validation samples).
\end{itemize}

Each image is preprocessed to construct synthetic multisource inputs.
Specifically, images are split horizontally into top and bottom halves,
which are treated as complementary views.
Each view is then binarized, spatially downsampled, and transformed into a six-dimensional vector
by computing the mean pixel intensity along columns.
These vectors correspond to the synthetic multisource inputs $x_{\mathrm{top}}$ and $x_{\mathrm{bottom}}$ defined in Section~\ref{sec:problem_formulation}.

An overview of the preprocessing pipeline is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:preprocessing}.

\section{Approach}

\subsection{Classical Neural Network Baseline}

\subsubsection{Model Architecture}
As a baseline, we employ a multilayer perceptron (MLP) architecture (Fig.~\ref{fig:mlp_diagram}).
Two feature extractors independently process $x_{\mathrm{top}}$ and $x_{\mathrm{bottom}}$, and their outputs are concatenated and trained end-to-end with the classifier.
Each feature extractor is an MLP with six input neurons, corresponding to the dimensionality of the input vectors, and a single hidden layer with ReLU activation.

The classifier consists of an input layer of size equal to the concatenated feature outputs, a hidden layer with ReLU activation, and an output layer with three neurons, corresponding to the number of target classes.
Training is performed using the cross-entropy loss, and predictions are obtained by selecting the output logit with the maximum value.

\subsection{Hybrid Classical-Quantum Neural Network}
For the hybrid approach, we replace the classifier from the classical neural network architecture,
and add variational quantum circuit instead. Feature extractors are kept the same.
Extracted features are concatenated $v = [v_{\mathrm{top}}, v_{\mathrm{bottom}}]$
and used as input into quantum cicruit using angle embedding.
Angle embedding is shown in equation~\ref{eq:embedding}, where $n$ is number of qubits --- equal to number of features fed into classifier ---
and $v_i$ is the i-th feature of feature vector $v$.

\begin{align}\label{eq:embedding}
	\ket{\phi(v)} & = U_{\mathrm{emb}}(v)\ket{0}                                                              \\
	              & = \left( R_X(v_1) \otimes R_X(v_2) \otimes ... \otimes R_X(v_n) \right) \ket{0} \nonumber
\end{align}
Where:
\begin{equation*}
	R_X(\alpha) = e^{-\mathrm{i} \alpha \sigma_X / 2}
\end{equation*}

Embedded features go through parametrized evolution called ansatz.
The ansatz used in the circuit is constructed from $L$ Basic Entangler Layers provided by the PennyLane package.
Trainable parameters used in ansatz are labeld as $\theta$.
\begin{equation}
	U_{\mathrm{ansatz}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{\ell=1}^{L} U_{\mathrm{BEL}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(\ell)})
\end{equation}

A single Basic Entangler Layer is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	U_{\mathrm{BEL}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) =
	\mathrm{CNOT}_{n,1}
	\left(\prod_{i=1}^{n-1} \mathrm{CNOT}_{i,i+1}\right)
	\left(\bigotimes_{i=1}^{n} R_Y(\theta_i)\right)
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{align*}
	R_Y(\theta) & = e^{-\mathrm{i} \theta \sigma_Y / 2} \\
	\mathrm{CNOT}_{i,j}
	            & =
	\ket{0}\!\bra{0}_i \otimes \mathbb{I}_{j}
	+
	\ket{1}\!\bra{1}_i \otimes \sigma_{X_j}
	, \qquad i \neq j
\end{align*}

\begin{equation}
	\langle \sigma_Z^{(j)} \rangle = \bra{\phi(v)}U^\dagger  \sigma_Z^{(j)}  U \ket{\phi(v)}, \qquad j = 1,2,3
\end{equation}

The measurement results correspond to the expectation values of the $\sigma_Z$ operator acting independently on the first three qubits.
\begin{equation}
	\sigma_Z^{(j)} = \mathbb{I}^{\otimes (j-1)} \otimes \sigma_Z \otimes \mathbb{I}^{\otimes (n-j)}
\end{equation}
